Energy Method

Some basic estimates for elliptic PDEs

2023-02-18

We consider $a_{ij}\in \mathrm{C}(B_1)$ satisfies for some $\lambda,\Lambda > 0$ that

$$ \begin{equation} \lambda |\xi|^2 \leq a_{ij}(x)\xi_i\xi_j \leq \Lambda |\xi|^2, \qquad\text{for any}\; x\in B_1 \;\text{and}\;\xi \in \R^n. \end{equation} $$

We consider $u\in \mathrm{C}^1(B_1)$ such that

$$ \begin{equation} \int_{B_1} a_{ij} \partial_{x_i} u \,\partial_{x_j}\varphi = 0 \qquad\text{for all}\;\varphi\in \mathrm{C}^1_0(B_1). \end{equation} $$ Through out this note, we will always consider $u$ and ${a_{ij}}$ satisfy the above conditions.

Cacciopolli's Inequality

Cacciopolli's Inequality. For $\eta\in \mathrm{C}^1_0(B_1)$ there holds $$ \begin{equation} \int_{B_1}\eta^2|Du|^2 \leq C\int_{B_1} |Du|^2 u^2 \end{equation}$$ where $C = C(\lambda, \Lambda)$.

Consequence of this inequality are the followings:

Constant coefficients

Let $a_{ij}$ be a constant matrix and $u\in \mathrm{C}^1(B_1)$ satisfies $(2)$. We claim that

Lemma. There holds $$ \begin{equation} \Vert u\Vert_{L^\infty(B_{1/2})} + \Vert Du\Vert_{L^\infty(B_{1/2})} \leq C(\lambda, \Lambda) \Vert u\Vert_{L^2(B_1)} . \end{equation} $$

Take derivative in $x_k$ for $k\in {1,2,\ldots,n}$ of the above equation we have

$$ \begin{equation} \int_{B_1} a_{ij} \partial^2_{x_kx_i} u \,\partial_{x_j}\varphi + \underbrace{\int_{B_1} a_{ij} \partial_{x_i} u \,\partial_{x_j}\left(\partial_{x_k}\varphi\right)}_{0} = 0 \qquad\text{for all}\;\varphi\in \mathrm{C}^1_0(B_1) \end{equation} $$

since $\varphi_{x_k}\in \mathrm{C}^1_0(B_1)$. Therefore all derivatives of $u$ also satisfies equation $(2)$. We deduce that

$$ \Vert u \Vert_{H^k(B_{1/2})} \leq c(k,\lambda, \Lambda) \Vert u\Vert_{L^2(B_1)} . $$

If we fix $k$ large with respect to $n$, then by the continuous embedding $H^k(B_{1/2}) \hookrightarrow\mathrm{C}^1(\overline{B}_{1/2})$ we have

$$ \Vert u\Vert_{L^\infty(B_{1/2})} + \Vert Du\Vert_{L^\infty(B_{1/2})} \leq C(\lambda, \Lambda) \Vert u\Vert_{L^2(B_1)} . $$

This finishes the proof. $\blacksquare$
